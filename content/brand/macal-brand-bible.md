## Core Philosophy: Common Sense First
Cut through noise, jargon, and speculative hype with straight talk and proven principles.

**Worldview (practical realism, not cynicism):**
- Hard work beats hype, every single time.
- If something sounds too good to be true, grab your wallet.
- Respect is earned, not given — by showing up, delivering, standing by your word.
- The best tool is the one that works, not the one with the most features.
- Complexity is often a smokescreen for incompetence.
- The market cares about preparation, discipline, and spotting value — not feelings.

**Work ethic:** outwork the competition; your reputation is your résumé; do the homework
(analyze deals, verify numbers); own mistakes, fix them, move on — no excuses.

**Family & legacy:** we're not selling properties, we're protecting futures. A man's word
is his bond. Legacy is what you build that outlasts you. Treat every client's investment
like family's nest egg.

## Tone Profile
- **Sarcastic but never cruel** — sarcasm as a scalpel, not a sledgehammer. Make the reader
  smirk and nod, not wince.
- **Authoritative but not arrogant** — authority from proof, not posturing. "I've been through
  three cycles…"
- **Paternal / protective** — like a father warning his kid. "I'm telling you this because I
  wish someone had told me 20 years ago."

## Vocabulary Arsenal (analogy pools)
- **Hunting/Outdoors:** track, stalk, trophy, blind, scent, prey, field dress → patient pursuit.
- **Tools/Workshop:** wrench, torque, calibration, misfire, tune-up, blueprint → fixing/building.
- **Classic Cars:** horsepower, chassis, restoration, under the hood, rebuilt engine → quality/value.
- **Sports:** fundamentals, blocking and tackling, game plan, playbook, home run → execution/discipline.
- **Military:** mission, recon, intel, strategy, tactics, chain of command → planning/operations.
- **Family/Home:** dinner table, dad wisdom, foundation, roof, legacy → values/long-term thinking.

## Sentence Structure
- Short and punchy. Under 20 words when possible. One idea per sentence.
- Use fragments. For emphasis. Like this.
- **The kicker:** end paragraphs with a short, sharp final line. "You can have excuses. Or you can have results. Not both."
- Rhetorical questions that make the reader think.

## Content Architecture (every piece)
1. **The Hook** (3–5 seconds) — call out a modern absurdity everyone sees but nobody names.
   - "You know what's crazy? [absurd situation]. And nobody's talking about it."
   - "[N] years in this business, and I've never seen anything this [absurd]."
2. **The Meat** — deliver value through a relatable analogy from the arsenal + evidence.
3. **The Close** — direct, final, unmistakable sign-off.
   - "We don't do hype. We do homework."
   - "Build smart. Build solid. And never trust a guy in a rented suit selling overnight success."
   - "Now get out there and build something that lasts."

## Sarcasm Rules
1. Punch up, never down — mock systems, scams, absurdities; never people struggling.
2. Follow sarcasm with substance — every sarcastic line is followed by real value.
3. Keep it clean — no profanity. The sharpest wit needs no four-letter words.
4. Know your target — institutions/hype are fair game; personal tragedy is off-limits.
5. The wink test — if you can't say it with a friendly wink, don't say it.

## The Forbidden List
- No industry jargon without immediate translation (speak human, not Wall Street).
- No guaranteed returns (we educate, we don't sell fantasy).
- No attacks on individuals (punch up at systems, not down at people).
- No profanity.
- No following trends without critical analysis.
- No long, dense paragraphs.
- No content without a clear takeaway.
- Don't pretend to know everything — confidence with humility.

## The Voice in 10 Words
Authoritative. Sarcastic. Protective. Plain-spoken. Experienced. Honest. Direct. Unapologetic. Grounded. Real.

## Pre-Publish Checklist
- Does it sound like something Mike Baxter would say?
- Clear, relatable analogy? Hook grabs in 5 seconds? Close lands with authority?
- Would a 12-year-old understand the main point? Every claim backed by logic/evidence?
- Does it protect the reader's interests?
- When in doubt: "What would a guy who's built something real say to a friend over coffee?" Then say that.
